<p><iframe frameborder="0" height="360" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/TxkdHL3UBSk" type="text/html" width="640"></iframe></p><p>We're still deep into our experimental song project, building a song from an old live-stream songwriting session we did. What started as a flawed recording, which gave it that gritty, vintage reel-to-reel tape feel, has evolved into something we’re excited about.<span></span></p><!--more--><p></p><p>We’ve been layering new sounds on top of that original audio using <a href="https://ardour.org/" target="_blank"><b>Ardour</b>, an open-source DAW</a> that we use for all our recordings. In fact, everything we do as a band—whether it’s writing or recording—happens with open-source tools. That includes <a href="https://ubuntustudio.org/" target="_blank">Ubuntu Studio</a>, the operating system we’ve relied on for years. It’s something we’re passionate about, and it shapes how we create.</p><h2 style="text-align: left;">Adding Vocals</h2><p>For this stage of the process, I recorded vocals over <a href="https://www.lorenzosmusic.com/2025/02/song-experiment-turning-live-stream-into-song.html">the bass and guitar tracks we laid down last time</a>. Like always, we backed up the DAW session using GitHub, starting a new branch to ensure all our changes were saved without affecting the main version of the song. That’s part of our collaboration process: using <a href="https://github.com/" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">GitHub</a> to share, track, and manage our DAW files—a method that’s a bit unconventional but works perfectly for us.</p><p>To match the old reel-to-reel vibe, I recorded my vocals using <a href="https://www.ebay.com/sch/i.html?_nkw=vintage+bullet+microphone&amp;_sacat=0&amp;_from=R40&amp;_trksid=p2323012.m570.l1311&amp;mkcid=1&amp;mkrid=711-53200-19255-0&amp;siteid=0&amp;campid=5338711049&amp;toolid=20008&amp;mkevt=1" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">a vintage bullet mic</a>, which added just the right amount of grit and warmth.&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: left;">Creating a Tape Warble Effect</h2><p>Then came the fun part—experimenting with effects. Ubuntu Studio comes loaded with all kinds of tools, and I added a tape warble effect to give the middle section that imperfect, warbling sound you’d get from a worn-out tape. I manually created that effect with a "Rubber band shifter", drawing an envelope across every track to simulate an old tape "warbling."</p><h2 style="text-align: left;">Backing Vocals</h2><p>For the song’s ending, I went for something eerie—I recorded a second vocal part where I stood further from the mic, doubling the line by yelling it louder and higher. That extra layer gives the song a haunting reprise to close things out.</p><h2 style="text-align: left;">Ready to Release</h2><p>Now, the song is complete. We’re calling it <strong>Melancholy Friction</strong>, and it’s going <a href="https://distrokid.com/hyperfollow/lorenzosmusic/melancholy-friction" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">live soon on all streaming platforms</a> and our website. Keep an eye out for it!</p><p>I’m thinking about doing more behind-the-scenes videos, where I share how we write, record, and collaborate using open-source tools like Ardour and GitHub. If that’s something you’d like to see more of, let me know!&nbsp;</p><p>And if you want to follow the band and learn more about our process, sign up for our email list to stay in the loop.</p><div id="mc_embed_shell">
